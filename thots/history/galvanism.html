Frankenstein: Fact or Science Fiction

 

            We all know the story of Frankenstein; a mad scientist named Dr. Frankenstein decides to rob graves and assemble body parts to make his own human. However, while the experiment succeeds, there are some side effects, and his monster runs away to Antarctica where it gets depressed because no one will ever love it and commits suicide. An absolutely wonderful story, isn’t it? However, when 21-year-old Mary Shelley published the story in 1818, it had a lot more grounding in fact that most people realize.

            The story of Frankenstein really started with surgeon Luigi Galvani. Galvani was just having an average day at the office on January 26, 1781. While some accounts say that Galvani was in his lab on a stormy day during an electric storm, more accounts detail him dissecting a frog while a collogue nearby was using what we would call an electrostatic generator. While it is debated whether it was actually Galvani or someone else who touched the frog for the first time, when touched with the brass scalpel, the frog’s leg convulsed. It had been known for quite some time at this point that other animals could produce their own electricity, such as the torpedo fish, as described by Aristotle. However, frogs were not known to have any electrical properties, and so Galvani was perplexed.

            After more experiments, Galvani eventually came up with the conclusion that “Electricity was… the great vital force, by which the decrees of the understanding, and the dictates of the will, were conveyed from the organs of the brain to the obedient members of the body.” Then, six years later, Galvani found his nephew hanging frogs onto metal hooks on the balcony outside his lab, and noticed that when the hooks touched the rail, the frogs convulse continuously. Galvani eventually concluded that he had discovered a sort of “animal electricity” and that with the metal, he was simply connecting two separate tanks of fluid electricity inside of the animal. He named his findings Galvanism, after himself. 

            However, not everyone agreed. Alessandro Volta, a professor of physics at the University of Pavia claimed that the electricity did not come from the frog, but rather was being conducted through the metal touching the animal and set out to prove it. He ended up creating a sort of battery, and then proved himself correct, with his findings spreading across Europe in the early 1800s. However, by this time, Luigi Galvani had already died.

            If we jump forward a few years to 1803, we find ourselves with a man named George Foster. Foster had been having troubles with his wife, and so she and their daughter had moved back in with Foster’s mother-in-law. However, he was slightly annoyed at this, and so murdered his wife and daughter, and then threw them into the Paddington Canal on an early Sunday morning (probably- it may have been late Saturday night). Foster was tried and found guilty and was then sentenced to death by hanging. However, this was not considered the worst part of his sentencing by any means.

            This is where Galvani comes back into things. Remember his nephew? His name was Giovanni Aldini, and like his uncle, was very interested in the animal/electric connection. However, he had been experimenting on humans for some time, doing various “party tricks” such as making decapitated criminals sit upright and jolting corpses with electricity; enough so that he had gained a reputation, and Foster had attempted to stab himself to death before his hanging, as the experiments were considered a much worse fate than hanging, should one actually fully succeed in bringing a human completely back to life. Upon Foster’s execution, his body was promptly delivered to Aldini, who inserted metal rods into the mouth and ear of the corpse. A book about Newgate prison, titled The Newgate Calendar detailed what happened next; “on the first application of the process to the face, the jaws of the deceased criminal began to quiver, and the adjoining muscles were horribly contorted, and one eye actually opened. In the subsequent part of the process, the right hand was raised and clenched, and the legs and thighs were set in motion.”

            While it may have appeared to nearby watchers that Aldini was succeeding in bringing the corpse back to life, his experiments ultimately failed. However, Shelley heard about the experiments, and from this we have the masterpiece, which is Frankenstein, which rather than being a science-fiction novel, is much more a glimpse into what they thought might be the near future, should someone successfully complete the experiment.

            However, as the experiments grew to be considered more inhumane, they became less and less common, and are no longer practiced publicly, with most of the cases being medical emergencies, where people were being shocked back to life after going into cardiac arrest or having some sort of other medical issue, rather than as an experiment. The USSR did allow some experiments, led by Dr. S.S. Bryukhonenko at the Institute of Experimental Physiology and Therapy, where they brought recently deceased dogs back to life. However, because the dogs had only been deceased for a time of around 10 minutes, it was not considered truly bringing them back to life, and they were able to successfully go on to live normal dog lives and have cute puppies.
